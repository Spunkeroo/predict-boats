What Is a Prediction Market?

A prediction market is a type of exchange where participants buy and sell contracts based on the outcome of real-world events. The price of a contract -- expressed as a probability between 0 and 100% -- reflects the collective judgment of all participants about how likely that event is to occur. When the event resolves, contracts on the correct outcome pay out $1.00 per share, and contracts on the wrong outcome expire worthless.

The fundamental insight is elegant: when people have real money on the line, they think more carefully and share information more honestly than when they are just expressing opinions. The combination of diverse participants, real financial incentives, and transparent pricing creates a remarkably accurate forecasting mechanism that consistently outperforms expert panels, polls, and algorithmic models on most questions.

Prediction markets are not gambling in the traditional sense. Unlike casino games where the house has a mathematical edge, prediction markets are skill-based competitions where better-informed participants profit from less-informed ones. If you have genuine expertise, local knowledge, or superior research ability, prediction markets are a direct way to monetize that edge.

How Prediction Markets Work

The Mechanics of Trading

Each prediction market presents a binary question with a clear resolution criteria and a specific deadline. Questions must be unambiguous and verifiable -- there has to be an objective way to determine YES or NO when the market closes. Good questions have clear resolution sources (government data, official announcements, independent verification) that leave no room for interpretation disputes.

When you trade a prediction market, you are buying probability. A share priced at $0.35 means the market collectively believes there is a 35% chance of the YES outcome. You can buy shares at the current price and hold them to resolution, or you can sell them before resolution if the price has moved in your favor. This creates both a forecasting mechanism and a trading market.

Automated Market Makers

The Predict Network uses an automated market maker (AMM) system to provide liquidity at all times. Unlike a traditional order book where you need a counterparty to match your trade, an AMM allows you to buy and sell immediately at algorithmically determined prices. The AMM adjusts prices based on the volume of YES and NO shares purchased, so heavy buying of YES shares pushes the probability up, and heavy NO buying pushes it down.

This means you can always enter and exit positions instantly, even in lower-volume markets. The trade-off is that larger positions face more slippage (price movement due to your own trade), so large traders should be aware of the price impact of big orders.

Resolution and Settlement

When a market's deadline passes, the Predict Network resolves the market based on the pre-specified criteria. If the outcome was YES, all YES shares pay out $1.00. If NO, all NO shares pay out $1.00. Shares on the losing side expire at $0.00. Settlement is near-instant, and winnings are immediately available for withdrawal or redeployment into new markets.

Why Prediction Markets Are Accurate

The accuracy of prediction markets is well-documented in academic literature and real-world track records. A landmark study by Wolfers and Zitzewitz comparing prediction market prices to polling on political outcomes found prediction markets consistently outperformed both traditional polls and expert forecasters. The Iowa Electronic Markets, the oldest academic prediction market, has outperformed national polls in US Presidential election forecasting in most election cycles since 1988.

The Wisdom of Crowds

The aggregation of diverse, independent judgments tends to produce more accurate estimates than any individual expert. This phenomenon, popularized by James Surowiecki's book "The Wisdom of Crowds," works best when crowd members have diverse information and genuine independence. Prediction markets are specifically designed to maximize both: the financial incentive for accuracy ensures participants share their true beliefs, and the market structure aggregates those beliefs into a single price.

Incentive Alignment

The critical difference between prediction markets and polls or surveys is the financial stake. When asked "who do you think will win the election?" most people answer with their hope or a social desirability bias. When real money is on the line, participants become dramatically more rigorous. They research more, think harder about base rates, and update more readily when new information arrives. Financial incentives align participants' expressed beliefs with their actual beliefs.

Continuous Information Updating

Prediction market prices update continuously as new information becomes available. When news breaks -- a major manufacturer announces production cuts, consumer confidence falls, interest rates change -- the market price adjusts within minutes as informed participants react. This real-time information aggregation makes prediction market prices more current than any periodic survey or analyst estimate.

"Prediction markets aggregate dispersed information faster and more accurately than almost any other mechanism. They are one of the few institutional structures that actually make people smarter." -- Robin Hanson, economist and prediction market pioneer

Winning Strategies for Beginners

If you are new to prediction markets, here are the core strategies that consistently generate positive returns for skilled participants:

Strategy 1: Trade What You Know

The simplest and most important strategy: only trade in markets where you have genuine expertise or information advantage. If you are a marine industry professional, trade boating markets on predict.boats. If you are a professional makeup artist, trade beauty markets on predict.makeup. If you are a software engineer, trade tech markets on predict.codes. Your edge comes from knowing more than the average participant in that market.

Beginners often make the mistake of spreading too thin, trading markets in 10 different categories where they have no edge. Specialists who focus on two or three categories consistently outperform generalists because their information advantage is real and exploitable.

Strategy 2: Fade Overreactions

Prediction markets, like all markets, sometimes overreact to news. When a dramatic piece of news hits and a market price moves sharply in one direction, the question is whether the new information actually justifies that magnitude of price change. Often it does not. Skilled traders identify overreactions and take the opposite side, waiting for the market to revert to a more accurate assessment.

This requires a stable prior belief and the courage to maintain a contrarian position when the crowd has moved against you. The discipline to hold overreaction trades is what separates profitable traders from those who panic and sell at the worst time.

Strategy 3: Exploit Base Rate Neglect

Most people dramatically underweight base rates in their probability estimates. The base rate is the historical frequency of an event: how often have similar events occurred in the past? When people focus on specific recent information and ignore the base rate, prediction markets misprice events. A common example: markets routinely overprice dramatic scenarios (massive crashes, breakthrough discoveries) because recency bias and narrative appeal outweigh historical base rates.

If you come into a market with a solid estimate of the base rate, you will often find the market price diverges from it in predictable directions, creating exploitable opportunities.

Strategy 4: Hunt for Thin Markets

In high-volume, heavily traded markets, the collective intelligence of thousands of sophisticated participants makes it very hard to find mispricings. However, in lower-volume markets with fewer participants, a single well-informed trader can have significant impact. If you have genuine expertise in a niche topic and find a market in that niche with relatively low volume, your information advantage is maximized.

Boating industry markets on predict.boats are a good example: the general trading public knows less about boat dealer inventory dynamics and seasonal demand patterns than someone who works in the industry or is an avid boating participant. Your specialized knowledge creates real edge in these markets.

Common Beginner Mistakes to Avoid

Overtrading: Taking too many positions dilutes your edge. Concentrate on markets where you have real advantages.

Ignoring resolution criteria: Always read the exact resolution criteria before buying. Many losses come from markets resolving differently than expected due to technical criteria.

Anchoring to round numbers: Probabilities of exactly 50%, 25%, 75% are often lazy estimates. Dig deeper for more precise probability estimates.

Correlation blindness: If you hold YES on three correlated markets (all depend on the same event), you are less diversified than you think.

Ignoring time value: A 60% YES position that takes 18 months to resolve is very different from the same position resolving in 2 weeks. Capital is tied up; price accordingly.

Finding Your Edge

The concept of "edge" in prediction markets refers to situations where your probability estimate is more accurate than the market price. An edge of 10 cents on a $0.40 market (you estimate 50%, market says 40%) is a 25% implied return if correct. Over many trades, consistent edge compounds into substantial returns.

Information Edge

You have information that the market does not. This could be professional expertise, direct observation, or access to data sources that most participants do not use. A boat dealer who observes floor traffic daily before official sales data is published has a genuine information edge on boating sales markets. A marine surveyor who sees the condition of used boats coming to market has edge on used boat pricing outcomes.

Analytical Edge

You process public information more rigorously than the average market participant. You build formal models, track base rates systematically, and avoid cognitive biases that lead others astray. Many market mispricings exist not because participants lack access to the relevant information, but because they analyze it poorly. Systematic, rigorous analysis can create real edge even using only public data.

Psychological Edge

You maintain discipline under pressure: you do not panic-sell good positions, you do not revenge-trade after losses, and you do not let recent wins make you overconfident. Many technically skilled prediction market traders lose money due to poor emotional management. Maintaining psychological stability is a genuine, underappreciated edge.
